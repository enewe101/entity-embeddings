Thanks for helping us with our research! In this task, you’ll be searching for relational nouns. Let’s learn about relational nouns through examples.

Anatomy of a relationship

Alice and Bob are siblings. We can depict their relationship like this:

In this task, we’ll refer to the members of a relationship (e.g. Alice and Bob) as the relata (singlar: relatum). So in this case, the relationship is siblinghood, and the relata are Alice and Bob.

What is a relational noun?

Relational nouns are a common way of expressing a relationship. For example, to express the relationship between Alice and Bob we can use the relational noun sister:

Alice is the sister of Bob.

Notice how sister establishes the relationship between Alice and Bob, but it actually refers to one of the relata specifically, Alice. That's a key feature of relational nouns: they invoke a relationship between two relata, while actually referring to one of them.

Note: A noun is relational if it:
  1. establishes a relationship, and
  2. refers specifically to one of the relata.

Now consider the two sentences below. Is the word “ally” relational? What about “alliance”?

Eve is Graham's ally.

Eve and Graham have an alliance.

Both “ally” and “alliance” depict the same relationship. But “ally” refers to one of the relata (Eve), whereas “alliance” does not (it refers to the relationship itself). So, ally meets the two criteria for being a relational noun, but alliance does not.

What counts as a relationship?

Remember that there are two criteria for a noun being relational.

  • It has to refer to one of the relata, and
  • it has to establish a relationship.

So, what counts as a relationship?

Let’s get the obvious cases out of the way first. Kinship relationships are easy to spot: things like brother, sister, uncle, or relative. Social relationships are also fairly easy to spot, things like assistant, boss, confidante, employee, enemy, etc. Other kinds of relationships are a bit less obvious. We’ll discuss properties, functional relationships, relative parts, and roles below, so that you’ll know what to do when you see them in the task.

Properties aren’t relational!

Properties are actually a group of nouns that we don’t count as relational—we want to point them out so you know to label them as non-relational!

Properties include things like:

flavour, colour, weight, speed, texture, redness, creativity, excellence, size, temperature.

You'll have the chance to test out your ability to spot properties in the final bunch of examples.

Functional relationships

Think about the word “cure”—is that a relational noun? Well, every cure is a cure for some disease. A cure is inherently defined in relation to the disease, being the agent that removes the disease. Also, the word “cure” refers to one of the relata in that relationship, so cure meets the criteria for being a relational noun.

Relative parts

A noun is a “relative part” if it singles out some part of a whole based on how it relates (usually physically) to the whole. For example:

Tofino is near the tip of Vancouver Island.

There is a chalet at the base of the mountain.

These examples meet the criteria for being relational nouns because they establish a relationship between the part and the whole, and they refer to one of the relata (the part).

Now, we need to be careful when thinking about parts. Just because something is typically a part of something else, doesn’t mean its relational. It has to be a relative part, which means that it is defined in terms of a relationship to the whole. Consider “wheel”. Although wheels happen to often be part of a larger object (like a vehicle), the word wheel doesn’t actually establish a relationship as such.

Roles

Roles are nouns that can indicate a vocation or a position in a group or organization. For example, “baker”, “director”, “CEO”, and “blacksmith” are roles. Sometimes roles are relational, sometimes they are not.

Consider the role “CEO”—is it relational? It seems like it is: CEO establishes a relationship between a company and its chief leader, and it refers to one of those relata (the leader). But CEO can also be used non-relationally. For example, in the sentence “CEO salaries have skyrocketed in recent years,” CEO is not being used to establish a relationship. That’s what makes roles trickier.

Faced with examples like that, your job is to exercise your judgment and decide whether it’s more common for the noun to be used relationally, or not. We’ll look at some examples together, but first, let’s talk more about how we’ll handle grey areas in general.

Grey area

The truth is, many words can be used both relationally and non-relationally. To handle those cases, we let you choose from three labels: usually relational, occasionally relational, almost never relational. Here’s how to use the labels:

  1. If you think the word is used relationally more than 50% of the time, choose usually relational;
  2. If the word can’t be used relationally, or if such a usage seems a bit unnatural or is confined to a very specific context, choose almost never relational;
  3. If using the word relationally seems perfectly natural, but using it non-relationally is more common, then choose occasionally relational.

Revisiting roles

Now let’s come back to the question of roles, and the example of “CEO”. We think that when that word is used, it is usually used to talk about the leadership relation to a particular company, rather than the job title (without reference to a particular company). In other words, we think it's more often used relationally. So we would label that word as usually-relational.

In contrast, if we consider the role “janitor”, although it is possible that it can be used to describe a relationship (i.e. “Mr. X is a janitor for Cleaning Services Inc.”), that usage seems rare. In the case of Janitor, we think that the decision lies between the options occasionally relational and almost never relational. In this case, we would choose almost never relational, but a good argument could be made for the other; it’s a matter of judgment.
