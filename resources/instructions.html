Overview

In this task, our aim is to find relational nouns. Relational nouns establish a relationship.

To demonstrate the difference between relational nouns and non-relational nouns, let's look at a few examples:

Relational nouns Non-relational nouns
sister enemy classmate replacement predecessor colleague fever bread astronaut book wheel wire flavour
Notice:
Relational nouns characterize a thing by how it relates to something else.

Reference objects

As we noted above, relational nouns describe a thing by how it relates to something else. We call that something else the reference object. Have a look at the example sentences below. Notice how the ones with relational nouns also contain a reference object.

Relational Non-relational
Alice is Bob's sister.
reference object
Alice is the sister of Bob. 		Alice is a carpenter.
no reference object!
Alice is a woman.

Take note of these 2 important things:

  1. Saying that Alice is Bob's sister doesn't tell us about Alice herself, rather, it tells us how Alice is related to Bob (the reference object). But saying that Alice is a woman or carpenter does tell us about Alice herself.
  2. It wouldn't really make sense to just say "Alice is a sister" without making it clear who she is the sister of. In other words, relational nouns need a reference object to make sense. But non-relational nouns like woman and carpenter don't take a reference object.
Notice:
Relational nouns need a reference object to complete their meaning.
The easiest way to tell if a noun is relational is to think about how it's used, and to try to figure out what the reference object would be.

Implicit reference objects

Sometimes the reference object is implied, and isn't stated explicitly. This makes the task a bit more tricky.

The relational noun stranger provides a good example. Suppose that Bob said the following:

Alice is a stranger.

Here Bob himself is the reference object (i.e. Alice is a stranger to Bob), even though Bob isn't mentioned in the sentence. It's implied because he's the speaker.

Let's look at a few more examples

The man was a foreigner.

Foreigner is a relationship relative to a particular country. Usually the country isn't mentioned, and is assumed to be whatever country the speaker is in.

We went to Paris because it's the capital.

Here again, the reference object is implied. The speaker could have said "... because it's the capital of France", but they decided to omit it, probably because Paris is well-known to be the capital of France.

Notice:
If the context makes it clear, reference objects might not be explicitly stated.

What is not a relational noun

There are many nouns which name relationships directly, for example "friendship". While "friend" is relational, "friendship" is not! The difference is that "friend" stands for one of the members of the relationship, whereas "friendship" signifies the relationship itself. A key feature of relational nouns is that they always stand for one of the members of the relationship!

So, if the relationship between Alice and Bob is one of siblinghood, we can say that Alice is Bob's sister, and Bob is Alice's brother. But notice that we can't say something like:

Alice is Bob's siblinghood.

This sounds wrong, because siblinghood stands for the relationship itself, rather than standing for one of the members of the relationship. The drawings below show the idea:

 

brother, sister, and friend are relational, but sibnlinghood and friendship are not.

Borderline cases

Words often have multiple meanings, and sometimes a noun has both a relational and a non-relational meaning.

As an example, consider teacher. Teacher can signify a relationship of mentorship and guidance, as in:

He became my teacher.

Or it can simply signify the profession:

He has been a teacher for fifteen years.

When faced with a borderline case, think about whether the noun is more likely to be used relationally or non-relationally.

Let's see a few examples of borderline cases, along with what we think is the right answer:
